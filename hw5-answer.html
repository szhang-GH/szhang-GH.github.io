  1. We are going to establish the equivalence of two-sample t test and linear regression. Suppose we observe outcomes Y_1,\ldots,Y_n from two groups, with group indicator z_i = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{for } i=1,\cdots, n_0 \\ 1 & \text{for } i=n_0+1,\cdots, n_0+n_1 \end{cases} Note that z_i=0/1 indicates the group membership of A/B, and n=n_0+n_1, and \bar{y}_A, \bar{y}_B be the sample means in Group A and Group B. Let \mathbf{1} be the n \times 1 vector of ones, and \mathbf{J}=\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}'.

    1. Define a design matrix \mathbf{X} = [\mathbf{1} \ \mathbf{z}] and define a regression model \mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, where \boldsymbol{\beta} = (\beta_0, \beta_1)' and random error \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim N(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}). For Y_i in Group A, what is its distribution? For Y_i in Group B, what is its distribution? What are the interpretations of \beta_0 and \beta_1 in this model?

    Answer: For Y_i in Group A, we have z_i=0, so the model simplifies to Y_i = \beta_0 + \varepsilon_i. Thus, Y_i in Group A follows N(\beta_0, \sigma^2) distribution. For Y_i in Group B, we have z_i=1, so the model simplifies to Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 + \varepsilon_i. Thus, Y_i in Group B follows N(\beta_0 + \beta_1, \sigma^2) distribution. The parameter \beta_0 represents the mean for Group A, while \beta_1 represents the difference in mean between Group B and Group A.

    1. Estimate the regression coefficeint by \boldsymbol{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}. Show that b_0 = \bar{y}_A and b_1 = \bar{y}_B - \bar{y}_A.

    Answer: The least squares estimates are given by \boldsymbol{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y}. For our design matrix \mathbf{X} = [\mathbf{1} \ \mathbf{z}], we have \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X} = \begin{pmatrix} n & n_1 \\ n_1 & n_1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} = \begin{pmatrix} \sum Y_i \\ \sum_{i \in B} Y_i \end{pmatrix}. (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} = \frac{1}{n n_1 - n_1^2} \begin{pmatrix} n_1 & -n_1 \\ -n_1 & n \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{n_0 n_1} \begin{pmatrix} n_1 & -n_1 \\ -n_1 & n \end{pmatrix}. Then \begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{b} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{Y} &= \frac{1}{n_0 n_1} \begin{pmatrix} n_1 & -n_1 \\ -n_1 & n \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \sum Y_i \\ \sum_{i \in B} Y_i \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \frac{1}{n_0 n_1} \begin{pmatrix} n_1 \sum Y_i - n_1 \sum_{i \in B} Y_i \\ -n_1 \sum Y_i + n \sum_{i \in B} Y_i \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \frac{1}{n_0 n_1} \begin{pmatrix} n_1 (\sum_{i \in A} Y_i + \sum_{i \in B} Y_i) - n_1 \sum_{i \in B} Y_i \\ -n_1 (\sum_{i \in A} Y_i + \sum_{i \in B} Y_i) + (n_0+n_1) \sum_{i \in B} Y_i \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \frac{1}{n_0 n_1} \begin{pmatrix} n_1 \sum_{i \in A} Y_i \\ n_0 \sum_{i \in B} Y_i - n_1 \sum_{i \in A} Y_i \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{n_0} \sum_{i \in A} Y_i \\ \frac{1}{n_1} \sum_{i \in B} Y_i - \frac{1}{n_0} \sum_{i \in A} Y_i \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} \bar{y}_A \\ \bar{y}_B - \bar{y}_A \end{pmatrix}. \end{aligned}

    We get b_0 = \bar{y}_A and b_1 = \bar{y}_B - \bar{y}_A.

    1. What is the Var(\mathbf{b}), the variance matrix of \mathbf{b}?

    Answer: The variance of the least squares estimator \mathbf{b} is given by Var(\mathbf{b}) = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}. From part (b), we have (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} = \frac{1}{n_0 n_1} \begin{pmatrix} n_1 & -n_1 \\ -n_1 & n \end{pmatrix}. Therefore, Var(\mathbf{b}) = \sigma^2 \frac{1}{n_0 n_1} \begin{pmatrix} n_1 & -n_1 \\ -n_1 & n \end{pmatrix}.

    1. For Y_i in Group A, what is the fitted value \hat{Y}_i? For Y_i in Group B, what is the fitted value \hat{Y}_i? What is the SSE from the regression? What is the estimated variance of the error, s^2=\hat{\sigma}^2?

    Answer:

    For Y_i in Group A, we have z_i=0, so the fitted value is \hat{Y}_i = \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y}_A.

    For Y_i in Group B, we have z_i=1, so the fitted value is \hat{Y}_i = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 = \bar{y}_B.

    The SSE from the regression is given by SSE = \sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2 = \sum_{i \in A} (Y_i - \bar{y}_A)^2 + \sum_{i \in B} (Y_i - \bar{y}_B)^2.

    The estimated variance of the error is s^2 = \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{SSE}{n-2} = \frac{\sum_{i \in A} (Y_i - \bar{y}_A)^2 + \sum_{i \in B} (Y_i - \bar{y}_B)^2}{n-2}.

    1. What is the standard error of b_1?

    Answer: The standard error of b_1 is given by the square root of the corresponding diagonal element of Var(\mathbf{b}). From part (c), we have Var(b_1) = \sigma^2 \frac{n}{n_0 n_1}=\sigma^2 \frac{n_0+n_1}{n_0 n_1}=\sigma^2 \left(\frac{1}{n_0} + \frac{1}{n_1}\right). Since we estimate \sigma^2 with s^2, the standard error of b_1 is s_{b_1} = \sqrt{\widehat{Var}(b_1)} = s \sqrt{\frac{1}{n_0} + \frac{1}{n_1}}.

    1. Show the T-statistics for testing H_0:\beta_1=0. What is its degree of freedom?

    Answer: The T-statistic for testing H_0: \beta_1 = 0 is given by T = \frac{b_1 - 0}{s_{b_1}} = \frac{\bar{y}_B - \bar{y}_A}{s \sqrt{\frac{1}{n_0} + \frac{1}{n_1}}}. The degree of freedom for this test is n - 2, since we have estimated two parameters (\beta_0 and \beta_1) in the regression model.

    1. Suppose you have two groups A and B, where Y_{Ai} in Group A are i.i.d. N(\mu_A, \sigma^2) and Y_{Bi} in Group B are i.i.d. N(\mu_B, \sigma^2). You have collect data from n_0 subjects in Group A, \{y_{A1}, \ldots, y_{An_0}\} and n_1 subjects in Group B, \{y_{B1}, \ldots, y_{Bn_1}\}. Show that the two-sample T-test statistic for testing H_0: \mu_A = \mu_B is the same as the T-statistic for testing H_0: \beta_1 = 0 in the regression model.

    Answer:

         Let

    \bar{y}_A = \frac{1}{n_0}\sum_{i=1}^{n_0} y_{Ai}, \qquad \bar{y}_B = \frac{1}{n_1}\sum_{i=1}^{n_1} y_{Bi}.

    Under the equal-variance assumption, the pooled sample variance is s_p^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n_0}(y_{Ai}-\bar{y}_A)^2 + \sum_{i=1}^{n_1}(y_{Bi}-\bar{y}_B)^2}{n_0+n_1-2}.

    The two-sample T-test statistic for testing H_0:\mu_A=\mu_B is T_{\text{two-sample}} = \frac{\bar{y}_B-\bar{y}_A}{s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_0}+\frac{1}{n_1}}}, with degree of freedom n_0+n_1-2.

    The T-statistics in question (f) and (g) are equivalent.

  2. Interaction and Nested Models (mtcars)

    1. Interpretation: Interpret the coefficient for the interaction term. What does it tell us about the relationship between weight and fuel efficiency for manual vs. automatic cars? Based on this model, what is the slope of wt for automatic cars? What is the slope of wt for manual cars?

    Answer:

    • The interaction term coefficient is \hat{\beta}_{wt:am} = -5.298. This value represents the difference in the slope of weight between manual and automatic cars. Specifically, the negative sign indicates that the negative effect of weight on mpg is even stronger (more negative) for manual cars compared to automatic cars.

    • For Automatic cars (am = 0): The equation simplifies to \widehat{mpg} = 31.416 - 3.786 \cdot wt. The slope is -3.786.

    • For Manual cars (am = 1): The equation simplifies to \widehat{mpg} = (31.416 + 14.878) + (-3.786 - 5.298) \cdot wt = 46.294 - 9.084 \cdot wt. The slope is -9.084.

    1. T-test for Interaction: Test whether the interaction term is statistically significant at the \alpha = 0.05 level using the t-statistic from the model summary. State your null and alternative hypotheses, the test statistic, p-value, and conclusion.

    Answer:

    • Hypotheses: H_0: \beta_{wt:am} = 0 (Interaction is not significant) vs. H_a: \beta_{wt:am} \neq 0 (Interaction is significant).
    • Test Statistic: From the summary, t = -3.653.
    • P-value: p = 0.00199 (or approx 0.002).
    • Conclusion: Since p < 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis. There is significant evidence that the relationship between weight and mpg depends on the transmission type (i.e., the interaction is significant).
